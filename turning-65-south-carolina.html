Your Medicare Roadmap

What Happens When You Turn 65

Most Americans become eligible for Medicare at age 65. But Medicare isn't automatic for everyone — and the enrollment decisions you make in the first few months can affect your coverage and costs for the rest of your life. That's why we encourage you to reach out before your 65th birthday so we can walk through everything together, at no cost.

The Parts of Medicare

🏥

Part A — Hospital Insurance

Covers inpatient hospital stays, skilled nursing facility care, hospice, and some home health care. Most people get Part A premium-free if they've worked 10+ years paying Medicare taxes.

👨‍⚕️

Part B — Medical Insurance

Covers doctor visits, outpatient services, preventive care, and medical equipment. The standard 2026 premium is $185/month. Part B is optional but almost everyone should enroll.

💊

Part D — Drug Coverage

Prescription drug coverage from private insurers. You need this unless your employer plan has creditable drug coverage. Skipping it triggers a permanent penalty.

📋

Part C — Medicare Advantage

An alternative to Original Medicare that bundles A, B, and usually D into one private plan. Often includes dental, vision, and extra benefits. Not the same as Medigap.

Your Initial Enrollment Period

Your Initial Enrollment Period (IEP) is a 7-month window: 3 months before your birthday month, your birthday month itself, and 3 months after. This is your first and best opportunity to sign up for Medicare. Enrolling in the first 3 months ensures your coverage starts on time.

Still Working at 65? You May Be Able to Delay

If you have health coverage through your employer (or a spouse's employer) and the employer has 20 or more employees, you may be able to delay Medicare enrollment without penalty. However, the rules are specific and the consequences of getting it wrong can be costly. Call us at 843-509-2462 before making any decisions about delaying.

After You Enroll — Choosing Your Coverage

Once you sign up for Medicare Parts A and B, you have two main paths:

  • Original Medicare + Medicare Supplement + Part D: Freedom to see any doctor nationwide. Predictable costs. Higher monthly premiums.
  • Medicare Advantage (Part C): All-in-one coverage, often with $0 premiums. Uses networks. May include dental, vision, and drug coverage.

We explain both options side by side — in plain English — so you can make the right choice for your health, budget, and lifestyle. No pressure. No jargon. Just honest answers.
