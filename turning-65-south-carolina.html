The 5 Most Important Medicare Decisions When You Turn 65

When you turn 65 in South Carolina, you face a series of Medicare decisions that will affect your healthcare costs and coverage for the rest of your life. Getting these right from the start is far easier — and less expensive — than fixing mistakes later.

  • Part A: Most people get this free (if you've worked 40+ quarters). Sign up automatically or actively.
  • Part B: Medical insurance with a monthly premium ($185/month in 2026). Critical to enroll on time unless you have qualifying employer coverage.
  • Part D: Prescription drug coverage. Enroll even if you don't take many medications to avoid the permanent late penalty.
  • Medicare Supplement vs. Medicare Advantage: The biggest decision. Both have significant tradeoffs depending on your health and lifestyle.
  • Enrollment timing: Missing your windows creates permanent penalties and coverage gaps.

If You're Still Working When You Turn 65

If you or your spouse is still working and covered by employer insurance, you may be able to delay Part B and Part D enrollment without penalty. But the rules are specific — the coverage must be from a current employer (not retiree coverage or COBRA), and the employer must have 20 or more employees. Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

When do I need to sign up for Medicare in South Carolina?

Your Initial Enrollment Period (IEP) is a 7-month window — 3 months before your 65th birthday month, your birthday month, and 3 months after. Enrolling in Part B during the first 3 months means your coverage starts the first day of your birthday month. Waiting until after your birthday causes a delay. If you have employer coverage when you turn 65, you may be able to delay without penalty — but you need to understand the rules first.

Do I have to take Medicare when I turn 65 in South Carolina?

Not necessarily. If you have qualifying employer coverage from your own (or spouse's) current employer, you may be able to delay Medicare Part B without penalty. However, if you're enrolled in Social Security when you turn 65, you're automatically enrolled in Parts A and B. Retiree coverage, COBRA, and marketplace plans generally do not count as creditable coverage for delaying Part B. Call us before you decide — a mistake here can cost you permanently.

What Medicare plan should I pick when I turn 65?

The two main paths are: (1) Original Medicare plus a Medicare Supplement and Part D plan, which offers maximum provider freedom and predictable costs; or (2) Medicare Advantage, which bundles everything into one plan usually with lower premiums. The right choice depends on your health, your preferred doctors, how much you travel, and your budget. This is the most important insurance decision of your life — let us help you get it right.

What is the penalty for enrolling in Medicare late in South Carolina?

Part B late enrollment penalty: 10% added to your premium for every 12-month period you delayed without creditable coverage — permanent. Part D late enrollment penalty: 1% of the national base premium for every month without creditable drug coverage — also permanent. These penalties last as long as you have Medicare. Getting enrollment right the first time saves you money for decades.
