Why Part D Plan Selection Matters in South Carolina

Many people pick a Part D plan based on the lowest premium and assume they're done. But the monthly premium is just one piece of the puzzle. The real cost of a Part D plan is the combination of premium, deductible, drug tier copays, and whether your medications are even on the formulary.

Two people taking different medications in the same Lexington zip code can have completely different "best" Part D plans. That's why comparing plans based on your specific medication list is the only way to find the truly lowest-cost option.

The $2,000 Out-of-Pocket Cap in 2026

One of the biggest Medicare changes in years is the new $2,000 annual out-of-pocket cap on prescription drugs, effective 2025. This is a major benefit for South Carolina seniors who take expensive medications — previously there was no cap, and some beneficiaries spent $5,000–$10,000 or more per year on drugs.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

What is Medicare Part D and do I need it in South Carolina?

Medicare Part D is prescription drug coverage added to Original Medicare or a standalone plan. If you have Original Medicare and a Medicare Supplement plan, you need a separate Part D plan for drug coverage. If you have Medicare Advantage, drug coverage is usually built in. If you don't enroll in Part D when first eligible and don't have other creditable drug coverage, you'll pay a late enrollment penalty — 1% of the national base premium for every month you delayed.

How do I find the best Part D plan for my medications in South Carolina?

The best Part D plan depends entirely on the specific medications you take. Each plan has a formulary (list of covered drugs) and different tier levels with different copays. A drug that's Tier 1 (cheapest) on one plan might be Tier 4 (most expensive) on another. We use Medicare's plan finder tool to match your exact medication list to the plan with the lowest total annual cost in your area.

What is the Medicare Part D coverage gap (donut hole) in 2026?

The coverage gap has been largely eliminated as of 2025. Under the Inflation Reduction Act, out-of-pocket drug costs are capped at $2,000 per year in 2025 and beyond. This is a major change that benefits South Carolina Medicare beneficiaries with high drug costs. However, plan premiums and formulary differences still matter significantly.

Can I change my Medicare Part D plan in South Carolina?

Yes. You can change your Part D plan during the Annual Enrollment Period (AEP) from October 15 to December 7 each year, with the new plan effective January 1. You should review your plan every year during AEP because formularies, premiums, and drug tiers change annually. What was the best plan last year may not be the best plan this year.

What happens if I don't enroll in Part D when I turn 65 in South Carolina?

If you don't enroll in Medicare Part D when first eligible and don't have other creditable drug coverage (like from an employer), you'll pay a late enrollment penalty when you do eventually enroll. The penalty is 1% of the national base beneficiary premium for each month without coverage — and it's permanent, added to your premium for as long as you have Part D.
