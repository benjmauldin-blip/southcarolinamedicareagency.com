ACA Insurance for South Carolina Individuals and Families

If you're not yet on Medicare, the ACA marketplace is likely your best option for comprehensive health coverage in South Carolina — especially if you're self-employed, between jobs, or your employer doesn't offer affordable coverage.

South Carolina has multiple insurance carriers offering ACA plans in most counties, with four metal tiers at different premium and cost-sharing levels. The most important factor for most people is the premium tax credit — a subsidy based on your income that can dramatically reduce your monthly premium.

Mauldin Insurance Group helps Lexington and Columbia area residents navigate the ACA marketplace from start to finish. We calculate your subsidy, compare every available plan, explain the CSR Silver plan benefit (the most underutilized subsidy in the ACA), and make sure you enroll correctly to avoid subsidy repayment issues at tax time.

The CSR Silver Plan — South Carolina's Hidden Subsidy

If your income falls between 100% and 250% of the federal poverty level, you may qualify for Cost Sharing Reductions (CSRs) on top of your premium tax credit. CSRs are only available on Silver plans and can lower your deductible from $5,000+ to as little as $300. Most people who qualify don't realize it exists. We always check for CSR eligibility before recommending a plan.

Losing Job Coverage in South Carolina?

Losing employer-sponsored health insurance triggers a 60-day Special Enrollment Period for ACA coverage. This is one of the most time-sensitive situations in health insurance — call us at 843-509-2462 as soon as you know you're losing coverage so we can get you enrolled before the deadline.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is ACA health insurance and how does it work in South Carolina?

The Affordable Care Act (ACA) created health insurance marketplaces where individuals and families can shop for private health plans with standardized benefits. In South Carolina, plans are available at HealthCare.gov. Depending on your income, you may qualify for premium tax credits (subsidies) that significantly reduce your monthly cost. All ACA plans must cover 10 essential health benefits including preventive care, emergency services, and prescription drugs.

How much does ACA health insurance cost in South Carolina?

Your cost depends on your age, household size, income, and the plan you choose. Premium tax credits are available for households earning between 100% and 400% of the federal poverty level, and enhanced subsidies introduced in 2021 extend help further up the income scale. Many South Carolina residents qualify for plans under $100/month after their subsidy. We calculate your exact subsidy amount and show you every plan available in your county.

Who qualifies for ACA health insurance in South Carolina?

ACA marketplace plans are available to U.S. citizens and legal residents who are not currently enrolled in Medicare, Medicaid, or CHIP, and don't have access to affordable employer coverage. South Carolina did not expand Medicaid, so there is a coverage gap for some lower-income residents — we can explain whether you fall into that gap and what options you have.

When can I enroll in an ACA plan in South Carolina?

The Open Enrollment Period (OEP) for ACA plans typically runs November 1 through January 15 each year for coverage starting the following year. Outside OEP, you need a Special Enrollment Period triggered by a qualifying life event such as losing job-based coverage, having a baby, getting married, or moving. Losing employer coverage is one of the most common triggers — you have 60 days to enroll.

Should I use an agent or go directly to HealthCare.gov in South Carolina?

Using a licensed independent agent like Mauldin Insurance Group costs you nothing — agents are paid by the insurance carrier, not by you. But working with an agent has major advantages: we help you compare all available plans, calculate your exact subsidy, identify potential mistakes that could cause repayment of subsidies at tax time, and assist you with any issues that come up during the year. Going directly to HealthCare.gov leaves you on your own.
